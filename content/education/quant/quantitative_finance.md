**Quantitative Finance**

### Beginner Level

**What Is Quantitative Finance?**  
Quantitative finance uses mathematics, statistics, and computer science to analyze markets, price derivatives, manage risk, and build systematic trading strategies.

**Origin**  
Modern quant finance accelerated after the Black-Scholes option pricing model in 1973 and grew rapidly with computers, data, and electronic markets.

**Why It Matters**  
Quantitative systems power modern markets, from ETF creation and options pricing to high-frequency trading and algorithmic portfolio management.

### Intermediate Level

**Market Mechanics**  
Core tools include statistics, probability, optimization, stochastic modeling, Monte Carlo simulation, factor modeling, and machine learning.

**How Quantitative Finance Behaves**  
Quant models can perform well when relationships are stable but may break during regime shifts, crowded trades, or black-swan events.

**Key Data to Watch**  
Sharpe ratio, Sortino ratio, factor exposure, drawdown, turnover, slippage, model decay, and live-versus-backtest performance.

### Advanced Level

**Institutional Behavior**  
Quant hedge funds such as Renaissance, Two Sigma, and DE Shaw use massive datasets, advanced mathematics, and systematic execution to trade at scale.

**Professional Use Cases**  
Statistical arbitrage, factor investing, derivative pricing, risk modeling, portfolio optimization, and systematic execution.

**AI Interpretation in Systems Like Arkhe**  
ML Agent backtests and evolves signals. Risk Agent detects model breakdown. Swarm Agent combines quant signals with macro, liquidity, and technical context.

**Key Takeaways**  
Quantitative finance turns markets into an engineering problem, but risk management remains essential.
